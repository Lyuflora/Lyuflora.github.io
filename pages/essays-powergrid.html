Power Grid Game Analyse

A strategy board game with 2~6 players.

Players, managing resources, bid and build powerline networks in a race to supply the most cities with power.

Power Grid is a strategy board game with 2~6 players. Players play their respective power companies in the game, bidding for power plants and resources to build a city power supply network. Players need to allocate assets and resources to expand the power supply city as much as possible by occupying cities and building more efficient power stations. In the end, the power network contains the largest number of cities to win. It is full of calculations and trade-offs for the allocation of game resources. The game is dense and complicated. It is a game that involves plenty of players' decision-making skills, foresight, as well as mental effort.

The future market will give players a way to predict market trends, and predicting the power plants that can be bid allows players to make strategic decisions. The efficiency of power plants in the entire market has risen as the market refreshes, and low-energy power plants have been eliminated. After the power plants are purchased from the current market, the future market will add new power plant cards. Players can measure whether to bid and the given price, thereby avoiding blind investment and allowing players to naturally establish strategic short-term goals. For example, in the current market, there are power plants with an efficiency of 06, 07, 08, 09, and the future market will show power plants of No. 10, 11, 13, and 31. Players can predict that after the next power plant is sold, the current market will be supplemented by the No. 11 power plant. He prefers to compete for No. 11, so he can decide to wait for a round of bidding.

If the network is blocked by a city, players need to pay extra line fees, which can cause competition between players and thinking about route planning. The limitation of only 3 power plants in each city makes players want to occupy cities with dangerous terrain, consolidate their own networks, and obstruct the routes planned by other players. Correspondingly, if the route is accidentally cut off by the opponent, a lot of money will be spent on building the route. For example, a player, but he guessed that his next family wanted to build a power plant in Detroit. He chose to include Chicago in his network, so that the opponent would pay more before supplying power to Detroit. Building your own power plant step by step is the player's short-term goal for each round. If the competition fails on the planned route, the player must reconsider his development plan.

The rules of the game give full consideration to balance, treat superior and inferior players differently, and set the player's order of action. The leading player can choose the power plant first, while the lagging player can buy resources first-at a lower price. After the order is determined, resources are purchased in reverse order, which not only gives the upper hand players a chance to develop themselves, but also prevents the lagging players from getting too far behind. In addition, the profit gap of the game gradually narrows as the expansion progresses. The gap between the power supply levels of 0 and 1 is 22-10=12; between 15 and 16, only 138-134=4. The narrowing income gap gives players a more competitive atmosphere, instead of letting the dominant players easily hit other players.
